01 — Discover
Research Brief

The Real Question Wasn't Technical

The project brief was to integrate a V2X collision prevention SDK into the TBM mobility app. But the actual research question was more fundamental: would everyday commuters understand, trust, and actually use a real-time safety alert — in a high-attention, real-world context like cycling through Bordeaux traffic?

Technical feasibility was already proven. Behavioral adoption wasn't. My role was to design and run the research to answer the harder question — and turn the findings into design decisions.

Research Objectives
Comprehension

Do users understand what each alert means — and what action it requires?

Acceptability

Is the alert behavior acceptable — appropriate frequency, timing and intrusiveness for real commuting contexts?

Daily Adoption Intent

Would participants actually keep the feature active during their daily commute?

Methodology

Real-World Simulation: 22 Participants, 2 Days

Rather than a lab study, I designed a field simulation putting participants in real commuting conditions on the streets of Bordeaux. The protocol combined a pre-test survey to establish baselines and recruit by persona, live scenario testing with the app running during actual journeys, and a structured post-test debrief capturing reactions, concerns and adoption intent.

Participants were recruited across three mobility profiles: cyclists, bus commuters, and car drivers, each with a different relationship to road safety alerts. 22 participants completed the full protocol over two days.

02 — Define
Key Insights

What 22 Participants Taught Us

I synthesised qualitative and quantitative data into structured findings, categorised by feature area, frequency and severity, then built a decision-ready stakeholder presentation translating insights into prioritised design recommendations.

INSIGHT 01
Alert language didn't match commuter mental models

Participants understood the urgency gradient (low, medium, high) but couldn't immediately map it to the required action. Technical severity didn't translate to behavioral clarity, particularly for cyclists who needed directional and distance cues to respond in time.

INSIGHT 02
Alert fatigue was the primary adoption risk

The most common concern: "I'll turn it off after the first week." Participants were willing to try the feature, but only if alerts remained meaningful and infrequent. A high-frequency system, even an accurate one, would be disabled within days. Trust and adoption depended on restraint.

INSIGHT 03
Users needed control — but not friction

Participants wanted to personalise their alert sensitivity, but rejected anything requiring active calibration mid-commute or at setup. The design implication was clear: personalization had to be immediate, one-tap, and intuitively understandable. Not a settings rabbit hole.

Analysis & Synthesis

Turning Raw Data into a Prioritised Roadmap

Every response from the 22-participant study was coded and aggregated in a structured Excel database. Occurrences were counted per finding, grouped by persona (cyclist, bus commuter, car driver), and weighted by frequency across the full sample.

Each finding carried a severity score and a persona tag, making it possible to prioritise not by instinct but by evidence: which issues affected which users, and how often. Selected recommendations were grouped by theme, integrated into the product roadmap, and presented to the broader V2X ecosystem at VivaTech.

03 — Develop
Design Exploration

From Insight to Interface

The core design challenge: how do you give users meaningful control over alert sensitivity without adding cognitive overhead — especially for cyclists who can't configure settings mid-journey?

First Direction

Initial concept: alert integration into TBM

The first iteration explored how alerts would surface within the existing TBM app UI: as inline banners, persistent states, or notification overlays, without disrupting the core navigation experience for users already in motion.

Design Exploration

Two approaches to protection level selection

From Insight 03, we knew users wanted control. Two competing interaction models were explored for how users set their alert sensitivity:

Option A: Slider

A continuous slider offering granular control, but requiring users to interpret abstract positions on a scale, adding cognitive load at the moment of setup.

Option B: Preset Tiers

Discrete protection levels with clear labels. Faster to configure and easier to understand, maps directly to mental models users already have for notification settings.

Decision →

The preset tier approach was chosen. The slider created ambiguity during evaluation — participants weren't sure what different positions actually meant in practice. The tier model matched existing mental models and required zero interpretation: one tap, immediately understood, set once.

Design Foundation

Designing Within the TBM System

The V2X integration had to feel native to the TBM app, not bolted on. Working from TBM's existing design system as the foundation, I ensured alert components, typography, color and spacing were consistent with the app users already used daily. For a safety feature, trust starts with recognizability.

Stakeholder Alignment

Weekly Validation: Design, UX Writing & Strategic Buy-In

Every design decision and every line of UX writing — alert copy, onboarding language, consent phrasing — went through weekly review sessions with all project stakeholders: Renault, Software République, Orange, Atos, Eviden, Eye-Net, TBM/Keolis, and the Ville de Bordeaux. These weren't status updates. They were working sessions that shaped the product.

This cadence was strategic. Integrating a V2X SDK into a live public transit app required explicit sign-off from every partner at every stage — technical, legal, and UX. By building validation into the weekly rhythm, we turned alignment into a delivery mechanism: no surprises at integration, no pushback at launch.

The result was a participatory launch. Every stakeholder had co-owned the decisions, which meant every partner actively promoted the feature to their users. Bordeaux's public transport network and cycling community launched together — making daily trips meaningfully safer for the people who use both.

04 — Deliver
Alert Design

Three Levels Designed to Be Understood at a Glance

Each alert level (informational, warning, high-severity collision) was designed with a distinct icon, color, copy and haptic pattern. Direction, distance and urgency had to be communicated in under a second, without the user looking away from the road.

Alerts in Context

Three real-world scenarios: a bus leaving a stop, a car overtaking, and a school zone. Each triggers the appropriate severity level based on proximity, speed, and road context. Direction, distance and urgency communicated in under a second.

Feature Onboarding

Introducing V2X Within the Native App

The TBM app already has its own native onboarding for new users. My role was to design a single integrated screen within that existing flow — presenting the V2X feature and letting users trigger a simulated alert to understand what to expect before their first real one.

Reducing first-encounter friction without adding steps outside the native journey. Validated with stakeholders for UX clarity and legal consent wording.

Account & Feedback

My Account & In-App Feedback Loop

The My Account screen gives users a persistent view of their protection settings and participation status, reinforcing the sense that they are active contributors to road safety rather than passive recipients of alerts.

The feedback popup closes the loop: after a real alert event, users can rate its relevance directly in-app. This data feeds back into alert calibration and builds a trust relationship between user and system over time.

Settings & Personalisation

Alert Fatigue Prevention by Design

The settings area lets users revisit their protection level and notification preferences at any time. Designed around the insight that trust erodes through overexposure — the tiered system gives users a clear lever to reduce alert frequency without disabling protection entirely.

App Integration

V2X Live in the TBM App

The final design integrates the V2X SDK seamlessly into the TBM mobility app. The solution is now live in Bordeaux — serving real commuters with contextual safety alerts during their daily journeys, without disrupting the core app experience.

Conclusion

This project showed what good UX Research actually changes. The question wasn't whether V2X technology worked. It did. The question was whether real users, in real conditions, would keep it on. By designing a field simulation that reflected actual commuting behavior, the research surfaced three insights that directly shaped every design decision: alert language, fatigue risk, and the need for frictionless control.

The weekly stakeholder validation sessions were just as important as the research itself. Getting sign-off on design and UX writing from seven partner organisations, at every step, turned alignment into a delivery mechanism. It meant the SDK integration happened without friction, and every partner arrived at launch as a co-owner of the product.

The result: a participatory launch across Bordeaux's public transport network and cycling community, making daily trips safer for the people who use both. The project won the SMCL Innovation Trophy in Low Carbon Mobility — and the solution is now live.
